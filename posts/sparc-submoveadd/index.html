SPARC: How Movement Smoothness is Measured

Companion simulation for the article on the SPARC measure of movement smoothness

Human movements are often composed of several smaller submovements — brief velocity pulses that combine to produce the overall motion. A healthy, well-practised movement tends to be dominated by a single smooth submovement. Movements impaired by neurological conditions, fatigue, or inexperience often show multiple submovements, producing a bumpier velocity profile.

SPARC (Spectral Arc Length) quantifies this smoothness by examining how spread out the movement's frequency content is. A smooth single-submovement has a compact, tidy spectrum; a multi-submovement movement spreads energy to higher frequencies, lengthening the spectral arc — and making SPARC more negative.

Purpose of this simulation

See directly how adding a second submovement changes both the velocity profile and the Fourier spectrum, and watch the SPARC value respond in real time.

How to use it

Drag the two sliders. The interval slider moves the second submovement later in time. The amplitude slider controls how large it is relative to the first. Set amplitude to 0 for a perfectly smooth single-submovement baseline.

What the outputs mean

SPARC
Spectral Arc Length. Always negative — closer to 0 means smoother. A single Gaussian submovement yields a value near −1 to −2. Multiple submovements push it more negative. The exact value depends on movement duration and spectral shape.
Duration D
Time between movement onset and offset, detected where velocity exceeds 5% of its peak. When you push the second submovement later, D increases — stretching the normalised velocity and directly affecting SPARC.
Cutoff fc
The highest frequency included in the SPARC calculation — the frequency beyond which the spectrum stays below 5% of its DC value. A multi-submovement movement has energy at higher frequencies, so fc grows, lengthening the arc.
